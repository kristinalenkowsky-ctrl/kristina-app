Framework Library
The academic edge — MBA frameworks applied to your search.
Your supply is scarce. No one else has 19 Broadway productions and a BNP Paribas ballroom. Know your leverage.Porter applied to candidacy
Competitive rivalry

Broadway-to-ops is a rare lane. Most candidates are pure EA or MBA-trained CoS. You occupy the gap between operational credibility and executive range.

Threat of substitutes

Could they promote internally or use a fractional CoS? Your letter must argue for why full-time and embedded is worth it — and why you specifically.

Buyer power

At $90k in NYC, firms have leverage. Counter with specificity: make it impossible to imagine anyone else in the role.

Your moat

19 productions. Union coordination. Live events with zero margin for error. That operational credibility under social pressure cannot be faked on a resume.

Formal titles and real power diverge. Read the org before you write the letter.Org theory applied
Four questions first
  • Who does this role report to, and how much actual authority do they have?
  • Is this in the decision flow or the support flow?
  • What happened to the last person here?
  • Does the posting say "partner" and "advise" — or "support" and "coordinate"?
How to apply it

Decision-flow: open by showing you understand the firm's operating model, not its task list. Support-flow: lead with execution excellence, then show the upside.

Their deepest fear isn't that you're unqualified — it's that you'll optimize for yourself once you're in the room.Principal-agent theory applied
What PE firms fear
  • Information leak to portfolio companies
  • Misaligned discretion in partner-level decisions
  • Social friction in tight, high-trust teams
What law firms fear
  • Confidentiality breach on billing and client identity
  • Wrong people seeing wrong information
  • Setting bad operational precedent
Your answer

Stage management is a principal-agent role by definition. The director leaves; you run the show. Broadway union culture is explicit about discretion, hierarchy, and who makes which call.

Most applicants write for HR. Write for the partner who has 45 seconds and has read 400 letters already.Executive communication theory
How a senior executive reads
  • Sentence 1: Am I being respected or flattered?
  • Para 1: Does this person know what I actually need?
  • Scan: numbers, named firms, real outcomes
  • Final para: Day one — or 90 days to ramp?
  • Length: More than one page wasn't edited
The one rule

Every sentence must survive: "Does this help me decide whether to hire this person?" If not — cut it. A memo to a peer beats a cover letter every time.

No emotional attachment to any single deal. Volume at the top, quality gates at every stage.Deal-flow applied to job search
Your funnel
  • Source: LinkedIn, firm sites, exec recruiters, referrals
  • Qualify: Sector, intensity, reporting line, comp
  • Outreach: Application + warm intro when possible
  • Screen: You evaluate them too
  • Close: Arrive with a 30/60/90. Most don't.
Conversion reality

$90k NYC finance/law ops: 100 applications → 15 screens → 5 final rounds → 1–2 offers. Normal. This tool handles quality. You need a system for volume too.
